Georgia's Weekly Unemployment Claims: US Department of Labor

By now we're all familiar with the scary charts of unemployment claims across the country. Regardless of state, county, or city, the dramatic spike starting in March of this year dwarfs all other weeks since the information was recorded. While the news is certainly bad, there are lots of questions left unanswered by simply looking at the weekly unemployment claims made in a particular state. By taking a closer look at the claims, we can determine which industries have taken the hardest hit and when. Not only that, but which communities have struggled the most on this front during the epidemic, and how might we keep track of any recovery for the labor market?

For starters, it's not entirely helpful for local leaders and organizations to view statewide workforce data. What goes on in Atlanta is not true for South Georgia, and vice versa. We need to make local data accessible to communities, in whichever geographic organization they need. Fortunately, Georgia's Department of Labor makes county level data accessible.

While the Metro Atlanta area has experienced the vast majority of claims, all 159 counties in the state have experienced a similar surge in unemployment claims since public health measures were taken to combat COVID-19. However, different industries have been impacted in different ways.

In addition to totals for counties, the state also makes weekly claims available by industry at the county level, making it easy to see which workers in which industries are now unemployed.

Certain industries like construction are more evenly spread across the state, which makes sense as construction projects have slowed with rising uncertainty. Manufacturers have been laying people off, which is particularly burdensome on communities in Northwest Georgia with a high concentration of manufacturers. The claims in education and government are seen everywhere, and could become more concerning as the year progresses with state and local governments facing substantial budget cuts.

Of course, viewing total claims only tells part of the story, and causes more populated areas to drown out the impact on more rural communities. Instead of total claims, we can look at the claims as a percentage of an area's labor force. Labor force metrics are published monthly by the Department of Labor, but we want to see claims since COVID-19 measures compared to the labor force before COVID-19. This can give us an approximation of how hard hit an area's total labor force has been by COVID-19.

Let's just look at the state as an example. In February of this year, Georgia had a labor force of over 5 million. Since March, nearly 2.2 million Georgians have filed for unemployment, as of May 29. That constitutes nearly 41% of the state's labor force before COVID-19.

Now, it's important to understand that this is just an approximation, based on estimates of that labor force in February. It also doesn't factor in the number of people that have come off unemployment claims, as there is a significant lag to that data's availability, which we'll get to later.
